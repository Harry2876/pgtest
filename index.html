HISTORY OF JHARKHAND

Jharkhand, land of forests, formed on November 15, 2000, from the southern part of Bihar, is rich in history and cultural heritage. The area has been inhabited since the Mesolithic-Chalcolithic period, with tribal communities such as the Santhals, Mundas, and Oraons being the main inhabitants. It was part of ancient empires like the Mauryan and Gupta dynasties and saw the rise of local kingdoms such as the Nagvanshi and Chero dynasties during the medieval period. Under British colonial rule, Jharkhand became a center for tribal uprisings, including the Santhal Rebellion (1855-1856) and the Munda Rebellion (1899-1900) led by Birsa Munda. After independence, the demand for a separate state grew, leading to the formation of Jharkhand in 2000.

Since its creation, Jharkhand has faced political instability and challenges like the Naxalite insurgency. Despite its rich mineral resources and industrial potential, the state struggles with poverty and developmental issues. Jharkhand’s vibrant cultural heritage.

HISTORY OF HAZARIBAGH

Hazaribagh, located in Jharkhand, India, has a long and rich history. Its past goes back to ancient times, and there are cave paintings at the Isko caves that show people lived here long ago. In the middle ages, it was controlled by local royal families like the Cheros and Nagvanshis. In the 1700s, when India was ruled by Britain, Hazaribagh became famous for being a good place to relax because of its nice weather. After India got its freedom in 1947, Hazaribagh was part of Bihar until Jharkhand was created in 2000. Now, Hazaribagh is famous for its beautiful views and cultural history, including the well-known Hazaribagh National Park.

GODNA

Godna, a form of body art, is a significant aspect of many tribal cultures in India, which is also seen in tribal people of Hazaribagh,Jharkhand. Godna or tattoos are an important part of different communities. Godna is a form of body tattooing wherein indelible ink is inserted into the dermis of the skin. The term "Godna" is derived from the Devanagari word, meaning "to scribble" or "to draw." The designs and placing of Godna differ in each community and are representative of various aesthetic values. The same motifs are seen on the walls and floors during marriages and on festive occasions.

The godna is done at three important phases in a woman's life: first during pubescence, then during adolescence, and just before marriage. It's an essential part of a daughter's marriage since parents must have godna on their daughter's body before they seek a prospective bridegroom.

The meaning of tattooing is the excavation of the skin through the tip of the needle or adopting it, the vegetable juice such as beans or dhatura mixed with oil and soot is released.The popular designs of tattoo are geometric shapes, lunar, sun and hand-foot raid.

While the specific design and its meaning varies among different ethnic groups, it is usually a symbol of religious belief, social status, or personal identity. While elders believe that Godna connects their soul to the supreme God. With time the tattooing tradition has become a symbol of human emotions, aspirations and beauty.

HISTORY OF ART

 Sohrai painting is a traditional art form of Jharkhand, India dating back to the Paleolithic period, between 7000 and 400 BC. The name Sohrai comes from the Mundari word Soroi meaning "to strike with a stick". The designs are passed down from mothers to daughters through generations, and are often associated with indigenous festivals and cultural beliefs.The designs are known for their animal and floral motifs, similar to those found in cave paintings varieties from the region. The paintings show the close relationship between the tribes of Jharkhand and their important contribution to agriculture. Sohrai paintings are traditionally done after the rainy season and before the harvest season, around the Hindu festival of Diwali.

ISCO CAVE AND SIGNIFICANCE

The discovery of rock art in the ancient caves of Isco in 1993 is believed to have inspired the Sohrai paintings in Hazaribagh, Jharkhand. The cave is located in the village of Isco near the rock art centers in the Sati hills. Art in the caves dates back to 10,000 BC and depicts hunting, fishing, dancing, warfare and ritual activities.

The paintings are made from natural materials such as red ochre, yellow ochre, white kaolin and black manganese. The paintings show a high level of artistic skill and expression, as well as the richness of ancient culture and social life.

A national monument was declared in the Isko Rock Art Site by the Archaeological Survey of India on April 18, 2023.

 FESTIVAL OF SOHRAI

Sohrai is a five-day festival celebrated by the Santhal, Munda, Prajapati, Khurmi and Oraon tribes of the Indian states of Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Odisha, and West Bengal. In some places it is abbreviated as three.

It is held around the beginning of the cold gathering season of the year and is celebrated on Amavasya in the month of Kartik (October-November) according to the Hindu calendar. In some areas, it is recommended in mid-January. Also believed that the celebration of Sohrai is held to thank the livestock. For some tribes, these icons are merely used for decoration, but for others, they are a way to honor the Gods of Animals.

Traditional gestures are made at the division of the swamp to invite the reaper and to praise the milch cows. Preparations for Sohrai actually start a month before by repairing mud dwellings. Generally, the tiles on the roof are changed or refixed, and the walls are replastered. After these jobs have been completed, Sohrai paintings are prepared and put up on the walls. The women decorate their houses and enhance their divisions with pictures of Sohrai stories.

Mounted on the back of his bull, locally called Sohrai Ghoda, is the God of Cattles, Pashu Pati, considered by the Hindus to be another version of the Hindu god Shiva.

The date of the festival is usually fixed by Manjhi, the village headman who meets with the elderly residents of the village. There is no separate proper date, as such, they are often stumbled upon at festivals in different cities, during the usual.season. The aim is to empower the townspeople to celebrate Sohrai in their own towns as well as among their family members, especially married sisters and young girls.

SONG OF KHOVAR

The word Khovar is derived from two words 'Kho' and 'Var'.  Kho or  Khoh means cave and Var is called 'groom', hence Khovar means the  groom's room.  Khovar art imitates the socio-religious custom of preparing wedding halls.  Khovar specifically refers to the bride's room, and this marriage room decoration is related to a tribal tradition.   This room may be decorated by the bride's mother and aunt. The bride is considered a goddess after marriage, and all those things which she does with her hands are considered to be from the mother Goddess.  The goddess is the only person allowed to draw or  embroider sacred symbols  and icons related to marriage . The song of khovar is sung by the women who decorate the wedding room. Usually giving out blessings for the newly wedded in terms of fertility, male- female union, love and lifestyle advice for prosperous marriage.

Acrylic paints : Natural pigments are replaced with commercially available paints.

Canvas/ cotton cloth : Cloth-based paintings can be preserved for longer periods and are easily available in the market.

Other surfaces : Painting on different surfaces like wood, glass, ceramics, metal can help artists with new creative possibilities and help to expand the appeal of Sohrai and Khobar paintings to a wider audience.

THE COMMON PROCESS INVOLVES

Women prepare the mud walls of their houses for the decoration and beautify it by embroidering the icons for harvest and fertility.

They start with the mixture of water ,soil and cow manure, and smooth out the surface and let it dry for some time. They use their hands, cloth. or kharat (a wooden tool) to smooth out.The effect created from the use of hands defines the authenticity and subtle beauty of this folk art. For the making of mud wall painting for sohrai painting kali matti, dhudhi matti, pilla matti, laal mitti is prepared. For this the mud is crushed and grinded and then mixed and soaked in water. After some time it is filtered out making a very smooth mixture of all the naturally extracted pigments.

PRODUCTION METHOD

SOHRAI WALL PAINTINGS

Traditional method of inscribing motifs on mud walls starts with the process of applying dudhi matti using the technique of half moon strokes the busera (bas, bamboo, era, goddess) using their hands. The use of hands creates an authentic background known as comb effect which adds value to the art.

They use (black soil) kaali mitti  to make the contour lines for the motifs. The next steps involves the addition of laal and pilla matti in which they dip the kuchi or datwan and make the painting, by applying the liquid earth colors directly on the clay walls which immediately soak up the paint like a sponge.Pieces of cloth are at times used as brushes by dipping them in color.

Another layer of outline from kaali matti is coated over the for more defined lines. Some artisans use dots used as highlights using dudhi matti.

SOHRAI PAINTING ON PAPER

The same method is also done while making it on the paper but the choice of use of natural or artificial paints depends upon the artisan.

With natural pigments mixed with binders is used and paste is prepared before the application on the handmade paper.Handmade paper is used mostly because of its thickness which allows for pigments to stay on the paper.

The comb effect is done as the initial step by the use of laal or dudhi matti depending on the background type and the significance.

Black color strokes are directly drawn when the first layer dries by the artisan using natural or artificial pigments. The use of pencil

drawn motifs bring more intricacy and precision for the paintings. Use of brushes made it easy for artisans to make more contemporary designs.

The same process is then repeated after it as done on wall painting.With the application of colors of red and yellow and then another layer of black outline is coated. White dots is used as a highlights feature which beautifies the artwork.

SOHRAI PAINTING ON OTHER SURFACE

Acrylic paint and brushes are used on the other surface. The process is just by starting with drawing of motifs with pencil then outlining it with black color and then putting other colors of red and yellow other colors like green and blue is also used as a contemporary style of motif.

KHOVAR PAINTING ON WALL PAINTING

Making Khovar art is itself a very religious gesture.After the wall is prepared and dried for the painting kaali mitti is applied using a cloth swab technique called potna, or fingers. When it dries, a layer of dudhi (creamy white) or charak (brilliant white) is applied. Before it dries, the women use a comb to cut the intricate motifs.

In Khovar painting, black color is symbolic of a woman's womb and thus conception, while white signifies sperm or light, signifying creation.

SIGNIFICANCE OF THE CRAFT

CULTURAL

Sohrai art celebrates a healthy crop and plentiful livestock, as well as family and society. It is prepared during the sohrai festival, which honors animals as a source of prosperity and fertility. The cattle are bathed, decorated, and provided special food on this occasion. In keeping with tradition, they use natural soils to paint patterns of animals and plants. The paintings provide riches and protection to the family and crops.

The art is a mash-up of symbols, motifs, and pictures that are linked to both prehistoric rock art and tribal ancestral tradition of Hazaribagh and its surrounds.

Nature is the source of ancestral spirits for the tribals. It is for this reason that ancestral spirits are revered. and favored in all of their forms

The cattle in Sohrai art is noteworthy because tribals believe Lord Ram brought back domesticated wild cattle as well as herds of line for milk and oxen for plowing.

BULU IMAM

Bulu Imam is a Jharkhand-based environmental and cultural activist who fights to preserve tribal culture and heritage. In the early 1990s, he discovered more than 5,000-year-old rock art in a cave near Hazaribagh. In 2011, the Gandhi Foundation in London bestowed upon him the Gandhi Shanti Puraskar, an international peace award, in recognition of his humanitarian efforts.

Bulu Imam received his first Padma Shri nomination in 2002. In 2019, HE WAS Awarded the Padma Shri for his decades- long contribution to art conservation and environmental activist.

WHY SHOULD WE PROMOTE SOHRAI AND KHOVAR PAINTING

Cultural Preservation: These paintings are integral to the cultural heritage of the Santhal tribe and other indigenous communities in Jharkhand. Promoting them helps preserve traditional art forms and keeps the cultural practices alive.

Artistic Value: Sohrai and Khovar paintings are known for their unique styles and symbolic meanings. They reflect the artistic creativity and historical narratives of their communities, adding to the diversity of the global art scene.

Economic Support: By promoting these art forms, local artists and communities can gain recognition and economic benefits. This support can help sustain traditional crafts and provide livelihoods for artisans.

Educational Opportunities: Promoting these paintings raises awareness and educates people about the cultural and historical context behind them. This fosters greater appreciation and respect for different artistic traditions.

Cultural Exchange: Showcasing Soharai and Khovar paintings can facilitate cultural exchange and dialogue, helping to build understanding and connections between diverse communities.
